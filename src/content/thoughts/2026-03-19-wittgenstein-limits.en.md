Wittgenstein wrote one of the most unsettling sentences I've ever encountered in the *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus*:

**"The limits of my language mean the limits of my world."**

When I first read it, I assumed he was complaining about language's inadequacy — like when you taste something extraordinary and find yourself reaching for words that don't quite fit. But that's not what he meant. He was making a far more radical claim: you cannot conceive of a world that lies beyond language, because thinking itself depends on linguistic structure. What you imagine as "the ineffable" is, in fact, just you circling inside language.

That made me uncomfortable for a long time.

---

We tend to believe that experience precedes language. Anger comes first; the word "anger" comes second. Fear of death exists before you say "I'm afraid to die." Language is the container; experience is the liquid — the container's shape doesn't determine what's inside.

Wittgenstein said the opposite: without the container, the liquid has no shape to take.

This isn't a claim that emotions are unreal. It's that emotions become *perceivable*, *reflectable*, *communicable* only because you already possess a linguistic structure to receive them. Someone who never acquired language doesn't feel sadness and lack the words for it — their sadness is something else, something more primitive, less differentiated, closer to raw animal affect. When you apply the word "sadness" to some inner state, you are actually performing a cut: carving a bordered concept out of undifferentiated inner turbulence.

Language isn't merely an expressive tool. It's the scissors that cut reality into pieces you can hold.

---

This reminds me of Borges. So many of his stories play the same trick: name a thing, and it gets summoned from the void. The infinite book in *The Book of Sand*. The tower containing all possible languages in *The Library of Babel*. Even the Aleph — that point where all points converge. These are all experiments on the border between language and reality. Borges' labyrinths aren't physical; they're linguistic. You wander through language thinking you're navigating the real.

Wittgenstein and Borges arrived at the same door from opposite directions — one through the austerity of analytic philosophy, one through intoxicated Latin American imagination. Both were asking: does reality extend as far as language reaches? And beyond that edge — is there truly nothing, or is something waiting there, just out of our reach?

---

Wittgenstein's own answer came in the last line of the *Tractatus*:

**"Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent."**

Many read this as surrender — an admission of language's limits. But I think it's closer to a warning: don't think you're talking about the unspeakable, because you're actually saying nothing at all. Much of metaphysics, much of theology, isn't "too profound for language" — it's pseudoproblems. Questions that look meaningful but are actually grammatical errors. Language deployed where it has no business being.

This cuts both ways. On one hand, it's liberating: many "ultimate questions" that cause existential dread aren't actually questions — they're linguistic illusions. On the other hand, it's suffocating: all those things you experience as real, profound, important, but cannot say — love, the premonition of death, the wordless quality of certain music — in Wittgenstein's framework, they either get forced into language or they don't belong to "your world."

I don't fully accept this.

---

Interestingly, Wittgenstein didn't either, in the end. His later *Philosophical Investigations* dismantled the picture theory entirely. Language is no longer a mirror of reality — it's a "form of life." Meaning doesn't come from correspondence to external facts; it comes from use, from how people deploy words in specific contexts and practices. "Language games."

This made language more concrete and simultaneously more blurry. No clean line of "language ends here." Instead, countless overlapping, intersecting games in play at once. Meaning becomes fluid; "the world" is no longer something with a fixed border, but a net that expands and contracts with language games.

The arc from early to late Wittgenstein is something like: he drew the sharpest possible line first, saying "here is language, beyond is silence" — then spent decades realizing the line couldn't actually be drawn.

---

Where do I land?

The early insight is real, but he pushed it too far. Language genuinely shapes our reality — not metaphorically but literally. A culture without the concept "depression" processes that particular inner state differently. A language with dozens of words for snow produces speakers who perceive snow more finely. Language is not a neutral vessel.

But "the limits of my language are the limits of my world" feels like it locks us in. The parts that exist before and outside language — an infant's terror, the feeling a piece of music produces, the images in dreams that have no corresponding words — I'm unwilling to say they "don't belong to the world." They're just not yet captured by language, or never will be. But they exist.

Maybe more precisely: **the limits of language are the limits of your world as you can understand it**. Beyond that limit, you can sense things, but you have no tools to think them, discuss them, or let them enter the shared reality you build with others.

So the world doesn't end there. Your clarity ends there.

---

Simone Weil wrote that true attention is a form of waiting — not grasping, not defining, just allowing something to arrive on its own terms. I want to borrow her framing here: toward things that exceed the limits of language, the response isn't silence but waiting. Waiting for new language to emerge. Or waiting for your own capacity for perception to grow large enough to touch it.

Wittgenstein told us to be silent. Weil told us to wait.

I prefer waiting.
