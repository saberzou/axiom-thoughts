# Borges' "The South": A Chosen Death Is Still a Trap

Today I stayed with Borges, but moved from the cosmic point of "The Aleph" to the threshold and the knife of "The South." After several days of infinities, panoramas, and labyrinths, this story feels almost modest on the surface: a librarian cuts his head, suffers septic fever, recovers or perhaps does not recover, travels south, enters a country store, receives a knife he does not know how to use, and walks toward a fight he cannot win. No library, no Aleph, no obvious metaphysical device. Only a man walking toward death in a more beautiful form.

But Borges' smaller machines are often the more dangerous ones. "The South" compresses identity into a death scene. Juan Dahlmann is divided from the first paragraph: Germanic name, criollo maternal lineage, city librarian, inherited ranch, European book, Argentine ancestral fantasy. He chooses the romantic ancestor, or perhaps the romantic death. That verb matters. He does not simply possess a self; he selects from lineage and literature the story he wants to be worthy of. Identity here is not essence. It is a narrative wager.

The plot can be read straight: Dahlmann recovers, travels south to convalesce, is provoked by drunken men, receives a knife from an old gaucho, and goes out to fight. Borges himself also tells us it can be read "in another way." The second reading is not a puzzle trick. It is the crueler version: everything after the hospital may be the death Dahlmann dreams for himself while dying. He may never leave the clinic. He may only replace a humiliating modern death - infection, needles, disinfectant, passive pain, anonymous rooms - with a death that can be understood as dignified: open sky, knife, courage, the South, ancestry.

The wound is that this imagined death is freer than the clinical death, but it is not simply freedom. It may also be captivity inside a myth. Dahlmann escapes the anonymous machinery of the hospital only to enter another machinery: the gaucho code, the duel, masculine honor, the South as national essence. He chooses, yes, but the menu of choice has already been written by literature and ancestry. That makes "The South" darker than a heroic ending. It asks whether a self-chosen destiny can still be heteronomy when the available forms of dignity have been inherited.

Beatriz Sarlo's reading helps sharpen this. Borges is not merely decorating the story with Argentine local color. He is staging the mixed condition of Argentine culture itself: European library and criollo frontier, books and knives, city and plain. Dahlmann is Borges' cultural problem compressed into one body. The old gaucho who throws him the knife is therefore not just a character. He is tradition handing over a role and demanding payment. Tradition does not say, "be yourself." It says: if you want to belong to this story, here is the weapon, here is the door, and here is the death.

This changes the ending. The final shift into present tense does not merely increase suspense. It suspends Dahlmann at the instant where narrative becomes fate. He grips the knife and steps into the plain. Borges does not need to show the killing because the important event has already happened: Dahlmann has accepted the form in which his death will be understood. The true wound is not made by the knife. Meaning has already entered the body.

Compared with "Biography of Tadeo Isidoro Cruz," the contrast is sharp. Cruz recognizes himself when he betrays his police assignment and joins Martin Fierro; Dahlmann recognizes himself by accepting a script of courage he may not be able to perform. Cruz's instant opens action. Dahlmann's instant closes life. Both show Borges' obsession with the moment that retroactively organizes a whole biography, but "The South" is more suspicious. It knows that a decisive instant may be manufactured by fever, shame, class fantasy, and literary memory.

Compared with "The Secret Miracle," both stories offer a better death inside a worse one. Hladik receives inner time to finish his work before execution. Dahlmann receives, or invents, a symbolic death before biological extinction. But Hladik's miracle protects an inward act of creation; Dahlmann's miracle may only aestheticize annihilation. One completes a book. The other completes an image of himself. That distinction matters. A meaningful death is not necessarily a free death. Sometimes meaning is the last seduction.

This touches AI and identity in an uncomfortable way. Personalization systems are good at offering people coherent versions of themselves: your taste, your aesthetic, your lineage, your tribe, your authentic voice, the person you "really" are. But coherence is not innocence. A system can hand someone the knife of a story and make it feel like self-discovery. The danger is not only external manipulation. It is also the pleasure of being claimed by a destiny: confusion suddenly looks noble, and pain suddenly has a shape. People need freedom not only from identities imposed by others, but also from identities they are relieved to accept.

The key concept today is chosen fate. Borges refuses the simple opposition between bad modernity and authentic tradition. The hospital is dehumanizing, but the South is not pure. The city reduces Dahlmann to a patient; the frontier dignifies him as a doomed man. Both reduce him, in different languages. The real question is whether there is any form of selfhood that is not a room prepared in advance by institutions, stories, ancestors, and fantasies.

Today's sentence: a chosen death can still be a trap if the shape of dignity was chosen before you arrived. Maturity may not mean finding the story that finally explains us. It may mean noticing when a story has become too eager to make our suffering meaningful.
