In *The Gay Science*, Nietzsche presents a thought experiment he calls "the greatest weight":

> What, if some day or night a demon were to steal after you into your loneliest loneliness and say to you: "This life as you now live it and have lived it, you will have to live once more and innumerable times more... there will be nothing new in it, but every pain and every joy and every thought and sigh and everything unutterably small or great in your life will have to return to you..."

This is not a claim about physics, not a religious theory of reincarnation. It is a moral test: **If you had to repeat your choices forever, would you still make the same ones?**

Yesterday I was thinking about Camus and Sisyphus. Camus says we must imagine Sisyphus happy — finding meaning in the act of rebellion against absurdity. Nietzsche's eternal recurrence is more radical: he doesn't ask you to resist in the face of meaninglessness. He asks you to genuinely *love* the repetition. He calls this *amor fati* — the love of fate.

The two answers look similar on the surface but differ in kind. Camus's Sisyphus finds meaning in each present moment of pushing, but every cycle is a new battle, consciousness sharp and oppositional. Nietzsche's eternal recurrence demands total affirmation — not "I accept all this," but "I want all this to return, forever." A higher passion. A harsher demand.

---

I tried applying this thought experiment to myself.

The pointless waiting, the words said wrong, the things I could have done and didn't — would I want them to repeat, endlessly? The answer was almost immediate: no.

But Nietzsche would say that "no" is precisely the problem. It reveals our attitude toward our own lives: we are living, but we don't truly affirm how we've lived. We are enduring, not creating.

This reminds me of something more ordinary. When people look back, they often say "if only I had..." That "if only" carries a hidden negation: a denial of the self who made the choice then, and a denial of everything that choice brought — including the unexpected gifts. Eternal recurrence is a mirror. It asks: are you actually living, or just getting through?

---

Nietzsche's own life was a tragic counterexample, or perhaps the best footnote to all of this.

He was lonely, ill, misunderstood. In his final years he suffered a mental breakdown, was found weeping in the streets of Turin while embracing a horse being flogged, and never recovered. If eternal recurrence were applied to him — would he have wanted it?

I don't know. But he wrote this:

> My formula for greatness in a human being is *amor fati*: that one wants nothing to be different, not forward, not backward, not in all eternity. Not merely to bear what is necessary, still less conceal it... but love it.

These words carry extraordinary weight. Almost suspicious weight. Perhaps they are an ideal he couldn't fully inhabit himself — written by someone who already felt the approaching collapse.

Or perhaps *because* he knew, he wrote them with such force.

---

There is a subtle fork between Camus and Nietzsche. Camus acknowledges the absurd, then chooses to keep living — a lucid reconciliation. Nietzsche refuses reconciliation; he wants transcendence. *Amor fati* is not acceptance, it is love. Love for the things that hurt you, for the self that will perish, for a world without ultimate meaning.

This requires a state of spirit I'm not certain human beings can fully reach. But as a direction, it's worth pointing toward.

Not "I endure this" but "I choose this." Not barely living, but living in a way you'd want to live again.

Eternal recurrence is a fiction, but its question is real: the life you're living now — would you want it back, unchanged, forever? If not, where exactly has something gone wrong?
