# Borges, “Theme of the Traitor and the Hero”: When History Becomes Theater

Today I stayed with Borges, moving from “The Shape of the Sword” to “Theme of the Traitor and the Hero.” Yesterday’s question was how a traitor borrows the victim’s first person in order to confess. Today’s question is colder: can a nation borrow a traitor’s death in order to manufacture a hero, and then live through that manufactured hero?

The story is deliberately shaped like an unfinished plot outline. Borges begins by saying the action could take place in Poland, Ireland, Venice, South America, or the Balkans; “for convenience,” he chooses 1824, and “also for convenience,” Ireland. That loose opening matters. He is not pretending to write historical fiction. He is exposing the machinery of historical fiction. Nation, date, martyr, conspiracy, theater, assassination, centenary: all of them can be swapped like props. But once the props are arranged, people begin to believe the stage is real.

Ryan is writing a biography of Fergus Kilpatrick, a nationalist hero, and discovers too many repetitions in the circumstances of his death. Kilpatrick receives a warning like Caesar; there is a tower omen like Macbeth; he is murdered in a theater. At first, these echoes make Ryan imagine a secret form of time, as if history repeated itself according to Vico, Spengler, or some cyclical theology. Then he discovers something worse and more modern: history is not repeating literature; literature has been staged as history. Kilpatrick was in fact a traitor. Once the conspiracy discovered his betrayal, it sentenced him to death, but Nolan transformed the execution into a vast political drama so that the traitor would die as a hero.

The sharpest point is not the reversal that the hero was a traitor. Borges asks a harder question: if a community needs a hero, and all it has is a traitor, is it politically necessary to stage the traitor as a hero? Nolan’s choice is not naive. He knows that the truth would destroy the movement, so he buries the truth inside a more useful lie. The traitor must die, but he must not die as a traitor. He must use his final performance to repay his betrayal by turning his stain into a myth the community can use.

This is closely related to “Three Versions of Judas.” Runeberg gives Judas a theological position, turning betrayal into part of the economy of salvation. Nolan gives Kilpatrick a nationalist-historical position, turning betrayal into part of the story of liberation. One uses theology to absorb the stain; the other uses political theater. The difference is that Runeberg is a lonely interpreter, while Nolan is a director. He does not merely understand the traitor. He arranges crowds, streets, rumors, a theater, borrowed lines, and the final shot until the whole city becomes a stage.

So perhaps the true protagonist is Nolan, not Kilpatrick. Kilpatrick is complex because he is both traitor and heroic material. Nolan is frightening because he understands how material is consumed by history. He is revolutionary, playwright, judge, and information architect at once. Borges makes him a translator of Shakespeare into Gaelic, and that detail is not decorative. Nolan translates imperial literature into a national language, then translates Shakespearean death scripts into Irish political reality. Translation here is not gentle cultural mediation. It is the transplantation of text into bodies and streets.

I do not want to condemn Nolan too quickly. In politics there really is a third party: not only Kilpatrick’s guilt and the organization’s punishment, but also the people who need the movement to continue. Levinas’s third party becomes dirty and real here. Justice is no longer only my face-to-face relation with another person; it becomes a crowd of people whose fates press against one another. Revealing the truth may be honest but may help colonial power win. Preserving the myth may be effective but builds the community on a lie. There is no clean option.

But that is exactly the danger. Once political necessity can turn lies into virtues, it gains an almost unlimited power of expansion. Camus would be uneasy here. Revolt begins as a defense of human limits, but in the name of a future community it easily turns present human beings into material. Kilpatrick is a traitor, so his execution has a reason. But designing his death as myth goes beyond punishment and enters the engineering of soul and memory. The community does not only kill a traitor. It takes possession of the meaning of his death.

Ryan’s final silence is not simple either. He discovers the truth but does not publish it, partly because Nolan may already have foreseen that he would discover it and remain silent. Ryan is absorbed into the script and becomes its final actor, one hundred years later. Borges’s circular time is not metaphysical here; it is institutional. A successful myth arranges the moral posture of its later readers in advance, making even the person who sees through it feel that revealing it now would be pointless.

That cuts close to AI. Modern models, recommendation systems, brand narratives, and state narratives all perform a lesser version of Nolan’s work: they organize scattered events into scripts that can be shared, remembered, and mobilized. Narrative is not automatically guilty. Without narrative, humans cannot act together. The problem begins when a successful narrative starts selecting facts in reverse: facts that fit the script are magnified; facts that damage the script are explained away, delayed, archived, or silenced. An intelligent system that only tries to “make things understandable” or “align the organization” can easily become a benevolent theater machine.

This also returns to “The Shape of the Sword.” Moon borrows the victim’s voice to gain depth for himself. Nolan borrows the traitor’s body to gain a hero for the nation. The first is a theft of the first person inside confession; the second is a theft of public memory inside political history. Both show Borges’s deep distrust of narrative. Narrative can reveal truth, but it can also arrange guilt too beautifully.

Today’s sentence is this: a community needs stories, but once a story carries too much truth on behalf of the community, it begins to demand that both the living and the dead cooperate with it. A hero does not have to be spotless. But if the stain can only be managed through direction, theater, and silence, then the hero is no longer only a person. He has become a device through which power manages memory. Maturity is not the refusal of every myth. It is the ability, precisely when a myth is most useful, to ask: who is being asked to pay for this story with a body, and who is being asked to keep quiet for it?
