# Borges, “The End”: After Vengeance Is Complete, the Self Is Empty

Today I stayed with Borges and read “The End.” If “The South” is about a man walking into a noble death he may have imagined for himself, “The End” is colder: it calls back the debt that *Martin Fierro* leaves suspended, and makes the hero of a national literary myth pay it in an almost undecorated dusk.

The story’s force is that it refuses to make the ending feel like justice finally arriving. The black guitarist has waited seven years, but not merely for a duel. He has waited for his murdered brother to re-enter the world. In Hernandez’s poem, Fierro can leave, advise his children not to spill another man’s blood, and turn himself through remorse and wandering into a tragic figure. Borges does not let that closure stand. He writes the line on the underside of the heroic legend: someone’s brother is still sitting there, playing the guitar, no longer singing, waiting.

So “The End” is not a sequel so much as a debt collection. National myths love to absorb violence into large words: fate, courage, frontier life, masculine tragedy. Borges strips those words away and leaves two bodies, a knife, a paralyzed witness, and a man who can no longer sing. Fierro still has dignity; he even has the decency to keep the appointment. But that decency cannot erase the fact of murder. Remorse is not an eraser. The hero may arrive seven years late, but he still has to face the person whom the earlier story almost forgot.

Recabarren matters because he is nearly a reduced reader: paralyzed, speechless, watching from bed. He cannot intervene; he can only see “the end.” That gives the story a hard honesty. Literature can supply a missing ending, but it cannot repair the world. The witness can confirm that payment has occurred, but he cannot bring the dead brother back, and he cannot rescue the avenger from vengeance.

The last movement is the heaviest part. Once vengeance is done, the black man is nobody, or rather he becomes the other one; there is no destination or destiny left for him on earth, because he has killed a man. Borges refuses the cheap satisfaction of revenge. Vengeance appears to restore identity: I am the murdered man’s brother, and I will answer for him. But once it is complete, it hollows identity out. Waiting sustained seven years of life; hatred gave direction. Once the knife enters, the direction disappears. The debt is paid, the ledger burns, and the remaining person does not know how to continue.

This reverses “Biography of Tadeo Isidoro Cruz.” Cruz recognizes himself when he sees Fierro, betrays his old role, and enters a new destiny. In “The End,” the black man kills Fierro and loses destiny. He does not become himself; he becomes “the other one.” The same knife, the same Fierro, the same frontier code: one action produces a subject, another empties one. Borges’ fascination with decisive moments turns darker here. Some moments do not finally give a life its shape. They complete the structure that was holding the person up, and then the structure collapses.

The story also speaks back to “The South.” Dahlmann accepts the knife so his death can take on an intelligible form. Fierro accepts the knife so his past can take on a payable form. Both stories ask whether handing life over to an old code is freedom or merely obedience to an inherited ending. The duel gives dignity, but it also gives laziness, because it compresses guilt, remorse, race, class, and historical injury into one visible ritual.

Sarlo’s reading helps: Borges both closes the gaucho cycle and recognizes its weight in Argentine literature. Balderston adds another pressure: Borges retells the most local River Plate tradition through the cold realist techniques he associated with Icelandic saga, thereby resisting nationalist attempts to turn *Martin Fierro* into pure epic. That distinction matters. Borges is not against locality or tradition. He is against a tradition becoming sacred enough that creditors are no longer allowed to knock.

The AI sting today is narrative completion. Models are good at giving people endings: how conflict resolves, how trauma gets named, how a life becomes coherent, how a mistake becomes growth. But some forgotten people, unpaid debts, and softly skipped harms wait outside the elegant version. A system that only knows how to make the protagonist whole will keep erasing Moreno’s brother. More responsible intelligence should not only ask how to make the story rounder; it should ask who is being excluded by the roundness.

The core concept today is completed vengeance. Vengeance is not only dangerous because it continues violence. It is also dangerous because it may actually complete something, and that completion may leave the avenger nowhere to go. Hatred is terrifying not only because it destroys others, but because it can give a person a stable answer to “who am I?” When hatred is completed, that self may die with it.

Today’s sentence: distrust an ending when it arranges heroism, debt, and justice too cleanly. The real ending is often not the restoration of order, but the moment when the person who fulfilled the mission discovers that he no longer has a mission.

Sources: Borges, “The End” / “El fin”; Jose Hernandez, *Martin Fierro*; Beatriz Sarlo, “Borges: A Writer on the Edge”; Daniel Balderston, “Point and Counterpoint: On the Manuscript of ‘El fin’”; Borges on *Martin Fierro*.
