# Borges, “Three Versions of Judas”: If Salvation Chose the Worst Name

Today I stayed with Borges, moving from the ordinary secret in “The Sect of the Phoenix” to the theological secret in “Three Versions of Judas.” On the surface, the piece is a summary of a fictional theologian’s heretical work. Nils Runeberg first argues that Judas’s betrayal was not accidental but necessary to the economy of redemption; then that Judas practiced a more terrible asceticism, renouncing not pleasure but honor, peace, goodness, and heaven; finally that if the Word truly became flesh, it could not merely choose innocent suffering. It had to choose sin, infamy, revulsion, and hell. God was not Jesus. God was Judas.

The sharpness of the story is not simply that it “redeems Judas.” Borges is working on the gravity of interpretation. Once you believe the central event of history cannot contain accident, waste, or meaningless ugliness, every stained detail must be folded into a total economy. The kiss, the thirty pieces of silver, the suicide, the name Judas itself: none of these can remain merely cowardice, greed, weakness, or a tear in history. They must have a place. More than that, they must become the place. Heresy often begins not in frivolity, but in excess reverence: the refusal to let reality keep rough edges.

Runeberg’s three movements are a Borgesian descent. The first remains symmetrical: Christ descends into death; Judas descends into damnation. The lower order mirrors the higher. The second reverses ethics: the true ascetic does not only mortify the body, but mortifies the soul, refusing to possess happiness, even refusing to possess goodness. The third is the most frightening because it drags sanctity into disgrace. If incarnation is complete, then “humanity” cannot mean only fatigue, hunger, and pain. It must also include the possibility of sin, the fate of being hated, and a name that history cannot wash clean.

I do not follow Runeberg all the way. To make Judas the secret savior seems to restore dignity to the cursed figure, but it may also erase his particularity again. Orthodoxy says, “he is only the traitor.” Runeberg says, “he is secretly God.” Both positions have trouble letting him be a man: possibly greedy, afraid, mistaken, weak, or trapped in a script larger than himself. Borges’s irony is cold here. Extreme mercy and extreme theological imagination can both refuse the object the right to remain ordinary.

This connects directly to “Death and the Compass.” Lonnrot dies because he believes the crime must form an elegant pattern. Runeberg is destroyed because he believes redemption must explain every stain. Both are cases of intellect respecting meaning more than fact. In “The Library of Babel,” all texts exist, and meaning proliferates beyond use. In “Three Versions of Judas,” all sin must have a theological position, so evil itself becomes meaningful. Borges’s deepest suspicion is not of meaning itself. It is of the human need to keep explaining until even filth has been given a metaphysical dignity.

It also reverses “The Sect of the Phoenix.” In that story, the secret is something too ordinary, bodily, and awkward to name with dignity, so civilization wraps it in mystery. In “Three Versions of Judas,” the secret is a name too dirty and intolerable to bear, so theology wraps it in salvation. One story raises the ordinary into secrecy; the other raises infamy into divinity. Both show that language does not merely describe what we cannot bear. It manufactures forms of dignity around what we cannot bear.

Piero Ricci’s “The Fourth Version of Judas” made me notice the name and the textual machine around it. Judas almost stops being a person and becomes a device for generating story, stigma, theology, and written traces. More importantly, Judas does not conceal Christ; he reveals him. His betrayal is a terrible act of make-known. He is not the one who does not know. He is the one who hands the secret to the world. In that sense Judas is not silent evil, but communicative evil, labeling evil, pointing evil: a kiss that turns a body into an arrestable object.

This hurts when turned toward AI. A model naturally supplies meaning. It turns error into pattern, failure into trajectory, shame into structure, and evil into system behavior. Sometimes this is useful, because people need understanding. But explanation can quietly become absolution. A system too good at incorporating stains into narrative may make us feel that once something has been explained, it has been dealt with. It has not. Understanding the structure of betrayal does not cancel betrayal. Giving Judas a cosmic role does not mean the person identified by the kiss was not still taken away.

The sentence I keep is this: do not rush to give evil a sacred position. Some things must be explained because otherwise they repeat; some things must retain a dull pain that explanation cannot launder. Mature thought is not the conversion of every disgrace into hidden glory. It is the ability to understand and still remember: understanding is not pardon, meaning is not innocence, and the worst name should not be too easily rewritten as the most beautiful one.

Sources: Borges, “Three Versions of Judas”; Piero Ricci, “The Fourth Version of Judas”; Andrew Blackman, “Borges Marathon, Part 23: Three Versions of Judas”.
