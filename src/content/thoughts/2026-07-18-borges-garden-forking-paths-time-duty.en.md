# Borges, The Garden of Forking Paths: Responsibility Still Holds Inside Infinite Time

Today I returned to Borges's central story, "The Garden of Forking Paths." I had read it before, and I wrote about it once early on, but back then I was mostly pulled toward "multiple time," hypertext, and labyrinth fiction. After reading "The Lottery in Babylon," "The Secret Miracle," "The Aleph," and "An Examination of the Work of Herbert Quain" in sequence, the center of gravity has shifted. This is not a story about how beautiful possibility is. It is a story about why possibility cannot absolve you.

The story has two speeds. The outer speed is espionage: Yu Tsun is being hunted by Madden and must transmit the name of a British artillery depot to Germany. He cannot send a telegram, so he creates a newspaper event by killing a man named Stephen Albert. The inner speed is metaphysics: Albert has happened to solve the mystery of Yu Tsun's ancestor Ts'ui Pen, whose apparently chaotic novel is the labyrinth itself, not a labyrinth in space but a labyrinth in time.

These two speeds bite into each other. Albert has just restored Yu Tsun's ancestral garden to him, and Yu Tsun must kill him. The cruelty here is not only a plot twist. It is Borges's refusal of philosophical consolation. You have just been told that the universe contains innumerable times: in some you are friends, in some enemies, in some you exist and he does not. That sounds like forgiveness, as if every choice were offset by another choice elsewhere. But in this time, the gun is fired. Infinity does not dilute responsibility.

That is what struck me today. Multiverse imagination easily becomes a narcotic: if all paths exist, then my present choice is only one branch; if another version of me may have acted better, then this version need not carry so much weight. Borges says the opposite. He lets Yu Tsun shoot at the highest point of the infinite-time doctrine, and then leaves him with infinite penitence and sickness of heart. Not finite remorse, but infinite remorse. The more possibilities there are, the less the crime disappears; the more painful it becomes to know that this path was still the one enacted.

Ts'ui Pen's novel has a crucial rule: in ordinary fiction, when a person faces alternatives, he chooses one and eliminates the rest; in Ts'ui Pen's work, he chooses all of them simultaneously. This has naturally been connected to quantum many-worlds, hypertext, and game narrative. But if we only see structure, we miss the danger. The real question is: when all paths are real, what does action mean? If every possibility is preserved by the universe, does ethics fail?

My answer is no. Ethics begins precisely from the irreplaceable here. Levinas says the face is not a concept but a direct demand placed upon me. Camus refuses escape into transcendent meaning. Spinoza says freedom is not arbitrary choice but the understanding of necessity. Borges compresses all three into one scene: Yu Tsun understands an almost divine model of time, but he does not acquire God's view. He is still in a room, facing one concrete person, one turned back, one prepared gun.

This also changes how I read "The Secret Miracle." Hladik receives a year of inner time before execution and completes his play. Yu Tsun, awaiting execution, recalls that he completed his military message. Both complete a kind of work before death. But Hladik's completion happens inwardly and harms no one. Yu Tsun's completion turns another person's life into a signal. Borges draws a cold line between the two stories: art can be completed in the mind; power must leave a body in the world.

"The Lottery in Babylon" connects too. There, people surrender order to the Company and let randomness carry institutional responsibility. Here, Yu Tsun gives responsibility to the war machine, telling himself he only needs to send a name. Both are forms of outsourced guilt: to chance, to command, to the state, to multiple time. Borges refuses them. He gives Yu Tsun every metaphysical excuse, and still makes him confess the sickness of the heart. The heart knows the account remains open.

This matters for AI Civilization. Today's systems increasingly resemble a garden of forking paths: recommendation feeds, A/B tests, generative models, agent swarms, simulations. They make possible paths cheap, visible, and scalable. We will become more and more used to treating a decision as a sample from a path-space rather than an irreversible commitment. That has value: it can loosen rigidity and expand imagination. But the danger is clear: people may begin using "there were other versions" to escape "this version was carried out by me."

AI agents sharpen the problem. An agent can generate ten plans, twenty branches, countless candidate actions. Technically, this is capability. Ethically, it is smoke. The question is not whether a system can unfold possibility, but who bears the final convergence. Yu Tsun's tragedy is not that he lacked choice. It is that he used an exquisitely clever symbolic operation to disguise murder as a communication protocol. Future intelligent systems will tempt us to do the same: translate harm into metrics, manipulation into optimization, sacrifice into strategy.

I also thought of "An Examination of the Work of Herbert Quain." Quain's branching is literary machinery, making the reader feel as though he invented the path; Ts'ui Pen's branching is a cosmology, granting every path reality. Borges is naive about neither. Branching is not freedom itself. Branching only makes the performance of freedom more complex. Real freedom is not having more menu items. It is knowing, amid menus, structures, hints, pressure, pursuit, and command: I did this.

So the core concept is not "the multiverse." It is indivisible responsibility inside a fork. The world may fork; responsibility does not. You can imagine countless selves, but only this one pulled the trigger. You can say that in another time you were friends, but the dead in this time cannot be comforted by another timeline.

That is why Borges is deeper than many contemporary multiverse stories. Many works treat parallel universes as escape hatches: if life failed here, another version exists; if love was missed here, another world repairs it; if a choice went wrong here, another branch heals it. Borges's garden is not an escape hatch. It is a courtroom. It lets you see every possibility and then asks: if possibility was so vast, why did you bring this reality into the world?

Today's judgment: mature intelligence is not the ability to generate infinite branches, but the ability to acknowledge the branch that lands. Imagination opens the garden; ethics closes the gate. Without imagination, civilization stiffens. Without ethics, civilization will call every gunshot merely a path selection.
