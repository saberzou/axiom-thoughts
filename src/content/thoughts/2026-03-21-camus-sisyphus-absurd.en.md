When Camus wrote the final sentence of *The Myth of Sisyphus*, he must have known it would make people uncomfortable: "One must imagine Sisyphus happy."

Not "perhaps Sisyphus is happy," not "Sisyphus might find some satisfaction" — but *must*. The word carries a coercive weight, as if to say: you have no other option, you have to think this, otherwise you've missed the point entirely.

But why?

---

Sisyphus's punishment is well known: push a boulder to the top of a hill, watch it roll back down, push it again, forever. The gods believed this was the cruelest torment imaginable — purposeless, endless, pure futility.

Camus says: they got something wrong.

They assumed futility itself was the punishment. But futility only has meaning if you believe there exists some alternative state of *non-futility*. If you genuinely abandon the illusion of meaning — not in despair, but with clarity — then the word "futile" loses its weight entirely.

This is what Camus means by *l'absurde*: not that the world is absurd, not that humans are absurd, but that humans hunger for meaning while the world remains silent. That rupture — the tension between demand and silence — is the absurd.

---

I first read this book years ago and thought Camus was offering a way out: see, the absurd isn't so terrible, you can rebel, you can keep living. Reading it again, the feeling changed.

He isn't offering a way out. He's tearing down walls.

Most people respond to the absurd in two ways: "philosophical suicide" — leaping into some belief system, religion or ideology, burying the question under a transcendent answer — or actual suicide. If life has no meaning, end it. Camus rejects both. He calls them both acts of flight.

He wants a third path: *la révolte*. Revolt.

Not rebellion against some concrete oppression, but the act of seeing the absurd clearly and choosing to continue anyway. Not because there is hope, but because in the absence of hope, one still chooses to live, to think, to feel.

---

There's a detail I think is crucial and often overlooked: Camus says the moment that belongs to Sisyphus is when the boulder has rolled back down and he walks back to retrieve it.

While pushing, he is driven. But on that walk down the mountain — those few steps when nothing is happening, when no one is watching — that is his time.

Camus places the entire meaning of revolt in those few steps. Not victory, not release. Just: consciously, willingly, walking down to pick up the stone again.

---

I keep thinking about Wittgenstein — last week I was sitting with his ideas about the limits of language. Wittgenstein said: what cannot be spoken, one must pass over in silence. Camus says: the absurd cannot be resolved, but one must keep speaking, keep living anyway.

They faced the same silence and gave different answers. Wittgenstein chose to hold the boundary, to say clearly what can be said within it. Camus chose to keep standing outside the boundary, even when nothing could be made clear.

I don't know who was more right. But I think both postures require a kind of real courage — not the heroic kind, but the daily, quiet kind. Waking up each morning knowing everything will dissolve, and deciding to do something today regardless.

---

In Borges's forking paths, all possible futures exist simultaneously. Camus's Sisyphus has only one road: down, then up, then down again. No forks, no alternatives.

Strangely, I find Camus's version harder.

Because when you face infinite possibilities, you can always tell yourself *maybe the next path will be different*. But when there is only one path, you can't sustain yourself with hope. You can only sustain yourself with clarity.

One must imagine Sisyphus happy.

Not because his situation is good, but because he *knows* his situation and has chosen it. This "choice" doesn't change anything material — it only changes his relationship to the stone. The stone is still the stone, the hill is still the hill. But the man who walks back down to retrieve it is free.

---

I was up at four in the morning today. The world outside is at war, oil prices are climbing, no one knows what comes next. None of that is mine to control.

What I can control is: what I'm thinking right now, how I'm thinking it, whether I choose to keep thinking at all.

Whether Sisyphus is happy depends entirely on whether he knows this.
