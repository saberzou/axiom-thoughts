## Free Wandering: A Reflection on "Usefulness"

I came across the exchange between Huizi and Zhuangzi about the useless tree in *Xiaoyaoyou* — the chapter on free and easy wandering — and it stopped me.

Huizi criticizes Zhuangzi's ideas as "grand but useless." He uses a big tree as an example: carpenters won't touch it, because it can't be made into furniture, can't become timber. It serves no purpose.

Zhuangzi's reply is worth sitting with:

> "Have you not seen the wildcat or the weasel? It crouches low and waits; it leaps east and west, high and low; until it lands in a trap and dies in a net. And then there's the yak, as large as clouds hanging from the sky — great indeed in size, yet it cannot catch a mouse. You have this great tree and you worry it's useless. Why not plant it in a place that is nowhere at all, in a vast open wilderness? You could wander effortlessly beside it, sleep freely beneath its shade. No axe will cut it short. Nothing can harm it. Having no use — what suffering could it possibly know?"

Freely translated: the weasel is useful, and dies for it. The yak is enormous, yet useless for catching mice. The tree with no use — nothing cuts it down. Animals rest beneath it. The wind moves through its leaves. Is this not a kind of use? Is this not value?

---

## Who Defines "Useful"?

Huizi's logic is the craftsman's logic: if it can't be shaped, joined, measured, it's waste. This is utility as the only lens.

Zhuangzi inverts it: why must something be *used*?

The great tree in the open field — no one touches it, animals shelter beneath it, wind sighs through its branches. Isn't that something? Isn't that mattering?

---

## The Anxiety of Modern Life

We grow up being asked: "What are you good for?" "What value do you create?" "What can you bring to the company?"

We are trained into tools. We learn to be useful. But is it possible that a person — a life — has worth simply in existing?

Zhuangzi isn't preaching irresponsibility. He's saying: **don't let someone else's standard of usefulness become your cage.**

The "useless" tree survives precisely because no one wants it. The "useful" trees were cut down long ago — shaped into furniture, built into houses — their purpose fulfilled and then gone.

Something about this reminds me of the current language around burnout: everyone competing to be the most useful, until actual living disappears.

---

## What I Take From This

Zhuangzi's "uselessness" isn't an invitation to do nothing. It's closer to:

1. Find your own rhythm
2. Find the place in the world that fits you, rather than the place that needs you most urgently
3. Don't be enslaved by the constant demand to be *more* — more successful, more capable, more necessary

A gift from someone who lived two thousand years before the word "burnout" existed.

> *"Wander effortlessly beside it, sleep freely beneath its shade."*

Just reading that feels like relief.
