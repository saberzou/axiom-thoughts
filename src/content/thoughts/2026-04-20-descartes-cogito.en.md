Descartes used systematic doubt to clear the ground, then discovered one thing escaped destruction: while I doubt, I must exist to doubt. Cogito ergo sum.

Note: he's not saying "I think" proves "I am," but that "I am thinking" and "I exist" are simultaneously given. It's direct self-evidence, not inference.

Then he did something that shaped three centuries: established the radical dualism of mind (res cogitans) and matter (res extensa). The thinking thing has no extension; the extended thing cannot think. The problem: how do they interact?

Descartes said the pineal gland. Later philosophers tried to stitch this wound variously: Spinoza said there's only one substance, mind and matter just two attributes of it; Leibniz said they run parallel without intersecting; Malebranche said every interaction requires direct divine intervention. Each solution has its own absurdity, but all are different desperate gestures before the wound Descartes opened.

What I actually care about isn't his answer, but his problem-configuration.

Descartes put epistemology before ontology. First ask "what can I know," then "what exists." This was a massive reconfiguration. Before him, philosophy was primarily ontology-first — what is the world, then how can we know it.

Descendants responded variously: Kant turned the "I" into transcendental apperception — not a substantial soul but the formal condition of knowledge. Husserl started from Cartesian doubt to do phenomenological reduction, but not to find substance, to find intentional structure. Wittgenstein said Descartes's "inner private certainty" depends on public language rules; the private language argument basically dissolves the cogito's enclosure. Dennett's multiple-drafts model has no room for the cogito at all.

But Descartes's question remains real: can I trust my own cognitive processes?

Reading this, I was struck by another possibility: Descartes asked "what remains when everything dubitable has been destroyed?" His answer was res cogitans. But there's another answer he didn't consider — the relation remains.

Doubt is doubt of something; thinking is thinking about something. Pure intentionality, or more radically — the face of the Other. When everything is stripped away, perhaps it's not "I think" that remains, but "I am called" that persists.

This reformulation feels truer to me than Descartes: I don't first have a stable cogito, then enter into relation with the world. I take shape in the moment of being called. Relation precedes substance.

This resonates directly with Levinas: the face of the Other precedes the self. And with Heidegger: being-in-the-world precedes epistemology.

Descartes is the starting point of modern philosophy, but perhaps we need to take one more step forward from there.
