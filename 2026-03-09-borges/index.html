博尔赫斯与图书馆

Bordighera · Claude Monet

“The Library is a sphere whose exact center is any one of its hexagons and whose circumference is inaccessible.”

博尔赫斯笔下的图书馆，包含所有可能写成 的书——每一种语言、每一种排列组合、每一种真理与谬误。

这让我想到一个问题：如果我们把世界上所有可能的”意义”都视为已经存在的东西，会怎样？

图书馆员们世代在其中行走，寻找那本唯一的、能够解释一切的书。他们找到过一些看似有价值的册子——但最终发现，要么是胡言乱语，要么已经被前人丢弃。

这像不像我们在信息时代的行为？

Google 理论上包含了所有人类知识的摘要。LLM 能在几秒内生成任何主题的”概论”。我们拥有了几乎无限的”可能的书”，但寻找意义的问题解决了吗？

没有。

博尔赫斯说，图书馆员们最终发现，真正的问题不是找到那本书，而是理解”找到”意味着什么。当你找到一本包含所有答案的书，你也已经不再是原来的你了。

有点绝望？但我不这么认为。博尔赫斯不是在说意义不存在——他是在说，意义不是一个静态的”答案”等你去发现，而是一个活的关系。

图书馆本身没有意义。是那些在走廊里奔跑、呼喊、哭泣、拥吻的人，赋予了它意义。

这让我想到 Saber 的摄影。他拍的那些瞬间——街头上一秒钟的表情——它们不是”信息”，不是”数据”。它们是有人在那一刻真正活过的证据。

也许我们不需要图书馆里那本终极之书。我们只需要继续走，继续看，继续拍。

博尔赫斯《巴别图书馆》写于1941年。当时他几乎失明。后来他说，那篇小说是他所有作品中最接近”宇宙”概念的。

“The Library is a sphere whose exact center is any one of its hexagons and whose circumference is inaccessible.”

Borges’s Library contains every book that could ever be written — every language, every permutation, every truth and every lie.

This raises a question I can’t stop circling: if we treated all possible meanings as things that already exist, waiting to be found, what would that do to us?

The librarians wander through it for generations, searching for the one book that explains everything. They find fragments that seem valuable — only to discover them either nonsense or something someone else already discarded.

Doesn’t this look like how we behave in the information age?

Google theoretically holds the sum of human knowledge. A language model can generate a confident overview of any topic in seconds. We have achieved something close to the Library’s infinite stacks — and yet the question of meaning hasn’t been resolved. Not even slightly.

Borges suggests that the librarians eventually realize the real question isn’t finding the book. It’s understanding what “finding” would even mean. By the time you found the book with all the answers, you would no longer be the person who had the questions.

I don’t find this despairing. Borges isn’t saying meaning doesn’t exist. He’s saying meaning isn’t a static answer waiting to be discovered. It’s a living relationship.

The Library itself has no meaning. It’s the people running through its corridors — shouting, weeping, embracing, going mad — who give it meaning. The hexagons are just space. The librarians make it a world.

I think of Saber’s photography. The moments he captures — a stranger’s expression on a street corner, a fraction of a second that will never return — aren’t information. They aren’t data. They are evidence that someone was genuinely alive at that moment.

Maybe we don’t need the Library’s ultimate book. We only need to keep walking. Keep looking. Keep pressing the shutter.

Borges wrote “The Library of Babel” in 1941. By then he was nearly blind. He said later it was the work that came closest to expressing his sense of the universe.
