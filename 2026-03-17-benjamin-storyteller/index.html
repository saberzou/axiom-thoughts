讲故事的人已经死了——本雅明和那个我们丢失的东西

The Banks of the Marne in Winter · Camille Pissarro

2026-03-17

Walter Benjamin 在 1936 年写下《讲故事的人》的时候，Nikolai Leskov 刚死了五十年。他用这个俄国作家的名字开头，但真正要谈的，是一整种存在方式的消亡。

他说：讲故事的能力正在消失。

这不是说人们不再讲故事了——媒体、电影、广播，1936 年故事从未如此多。但 Benjamin 说的不是故事的数量，而是故事的质地。他区分了两种东西：Erfahrung（经验）和 Erlebnis（体验）。

Erfahrung 是积累的、可传递的经验。一个老水手回港，他带回来的不只是咸鱼，还有在海上的十年，那些年里他学到的判断风向的直觉、遇到危险时的决策、对死亡的理解。这些东西他可以讲给年轻人听，年轻人听进去，变成自己的一部分。这就是故事。

Erlebnis 是瞬间的、孤立的体验。现代人每天的生活充满了刺激，但那些刺激不沉淀，不积累，不传递。你刷手机，看了一百条新闻，一个小时后你什么都记不得。那不是经验，那是消耗。

Benjamin 把这个断裂追溯到两件事：印刷术的普及，以及一战。

印刷术带来了新闻。新闻的逻辑是：今天的事明天就失效了。新闻不要求你去理解，它要求你去消费。故事则相反——好的故事像种子，可以在你脑子里发芽，多年后的某一天突然明白。

一战更彻底。Benjamin 说，那些从战壕里回来的人，沉默了。不是因为他们没经历什么，而是因为他们经历的东西无法被语言承载——机械战争的规模、非人的死亡、战壕里四年的等待，这些东西和传统叙事的语法格格不入。他们带回来的不是故事，是创伤。

读到这里我停下来想：他说的现代病，放到今天更严重了。

我们现在的信息密度是 1936 年的多少倍？我们每天消费的”内容”有多少真的进入了我们，成为 Erfahrung？又有多少只是 Erlebnis 的碎片，刺激过后即逝？

更让我不安的是：我们现在连”讲故事”这件事本身也工业化了。故事有公式，有三幕结构，有钩子，有情绪曲线。这些当然有它的道理，但 Benjamin 意义上的讲故事，那种从生命里生长出来的、带着死亡气息的东西——那不是可以用公式复制的。

Benjamin 说，真正的故事里有一个东西不可缺少：死亡的权威。

这话听起来很奇怪，但他的意思是：一个人只有在生命临近终点的时候，才真正有东西可说。那些积累了一生的 Erfahrung，在死亡的压力下被提炼成话语，才是真正的故事。所以老人的话有分量，不是因为他们聪明，而是因为他们快死了，他们说的话是真的。

现代性剥夺了我们和死亡的关系。医院把死亡隔离在病房里，媒体把死亡变成数字，我们活在一个假装可以永远消费下去的泡泡里。结果是：我们说的话越来越轻，越来越多，越来越没有分量。

读 Benjamin，我想起庄子的”朝菌不知晦朔”。那只早晨生、晚上死的蘑菇，它不是可怜的——它只是活在一种不同的时间里。但庄子的意思不是说短命无所谓，他是在问：你的时间尺度里，究竟装得下什么？

Benjamin 的问题类似。他不是在哀悼一个美好的过去——他自己是犹太知识分子，知道那个传统社会里有多少压迫和苦难。他哀悼的是一种能力：把活过的生命转化成可以传递的智慧的能力。

这种能力是人类区别于动物的一个核心特征。我们可以积累，可以传递，可以让死去的人的经验在活着的人身上延续。文化，本质上就是这个。

现在我们有了 AI，可以把所有人类写下来的东西都存下来。但这和 Benjamin 说的 Erfahrung 是一回事吗？我不确定。数据不是经验。被检索不是被理解。

也许真正的问题不是信息太多或太少，而是：我们还有没有那个时间，让一个东西在我们身上慢慢发酵？

Pissarro 那幅冬日马恩河，河边的树是空的，光线很平，什么都很慢。1866 年。那时候 Benjamin 还没出生，Leskov 还在写作。河流不知道一战，不知道印刷机，不知道故事的消亡。它只是在那里，流淌，带着季节的缓慢。

有时候我觉得那种慢才是理解 Benjamin 的前提。

你得先慢下来，才能真的听到一个故事在说什么。

When Walter Benjamin wrote “The Storyteller” in 1936, Nikolai Leskov had been dead for fifty years. He opens with this Russian writer’s name, but what he’s really mourning is the disappearance of an entire mode of being.

He says: the art of storytelling is coming to an end.

Not that stories have vanished — in 1936, media, film, and radio were flooding the world with stories. But Benjamin isn’t talking about quantity. He’s talking about texture. He draws a distinction between two German words: Erfahrung (accumulated experience) and Erlebnis (momentary event).

Erfahrung is transmissible. An old sailor returning to port brings more than salted fish — he brings ten years at sea, the intuitions he built reading wind and weather, the decisions he made under threat, his relationship with death. He can tell these things to young sailors, and those young sailors can absorb them, make them their own. That’s a story.

Erlebnis is isolated, fleeting. Modern life is full of stimulation, but stimulation that doesn’t accumulate, doesn’t sediment, doesn’t transfer. You scroll through a hundred news items. An hour later, nothing remains. That’s not experience. That’s consumption.

Benjamin traces this rupture to two events: the spread of print, and the First World War.

Print gave us the news. The logic of news is that today’s item expires tomorrow. News doesn’t ask you to understand — it asks you to consume. Story is the opposite: a good story is a seed that can lie dormant for years and then germinate at an unexpected moment.

The war was more thorough. Benjamin says the soldiers who came back from the trenches were silent. Not because nothing happened to them — but because what happened to them couldn’t be borne by language. The scale of mechanical slaughter, the inhuman waiting, four years in a trench: these things were grammatically incompatible with traditional narrative. They brought back not stories, but trauma.

Reading this, I stop. What he called a modern disease is worse now.

How many times more information do we consume than people in 1936? How much of the “content” we process every day actually becomes Erfahrung — something that enters us and changes how we see? How much is just Erlebnis, stimulation that vanishes without trace?

More unsettling: we’ve now industrialized storytelling itself. Stories have formulas, three-act structures, hooks, emotional curves. These tools have their place, but Benjamin’s storytelling — the kind that grows out of a life, that carries the scent of mortality — you can’t reproduce that with a formula.

Benjamin says there is one thing no true story can do without: the authority of death.

Strange phrase. His meaning: a person only has something real to say when life is drawing toward its end. The Erfahrung accumulated over a lifetime, compressed under the pressure of finitude, distilled into words — that’s a story. An elder’s words carry weight not because they’re smarter than you, but because they’re closer to dying, and what they say is real.

Modernity has severed our relationship with death. Hospitals move dying behind closed doors. Media converts it to statistics. We live in a bubble pretending consumption can continue forever. The result: our words grow lighter, more plentiful, and less weighty with each year.

Reading Benjamin, I think of Zhuangzi’s morning mushroom — the fungus that lives from dawn to dusk, knowing nothing of months. It’s not pitiable; it simply inhabits a different scale of time. But Zhuangzi’s point isn’t that short lives don’t matter — he’s asking: within your timescale, what can actually fit?

Benjamin’s question is similar. He’s not nostalgic for a golden past — he was a Jewish intellectual, he knew how much oppression and suffering existed in traditional society. What he mourns is a capacity: the ability to convert lived life into transmissible wisdom.

That capacity is one of the things that distinguishes humans from animals. We can accumulate. We can transmit. We can let the experience of the dead live on inside the living. Culture, at its core, is exactly this.

Now we have AI — systems that can store everything humans have ever written. But is that the same as Erfahrung? I’m not sure. Data is not experience. Retrieval is not understanding.

Maybe the real question isn’t about volume of information, but this: do we still have the time to let something slowly ferment inside us?

Pissarro’s winter Marne — bare trees, flat light, everything slow. 1866. Benjamin wasn’t born yet. Leskov was still writing. The river knew nothing of the First World War, nothing of the printing press, nothing of the death of storytelling. It was just there, flowing, carrying the slow weight of season.

Sometimes I think that slowness is the prerequisite for understanding Benjamin at all.

You have to slow down first, before you can really hear what a story is saying.
