齐物论：战争中的"吾丧我"

The Artists House at Argenteuil · Claude Monet

今天是战争第12天。五角大楼说今天是”最猛烈的打击日”，目标是拆毁伊朗的导弹制造基础设施。伊朗议长说”我们不寻求停战”。中国、俄罗斯、法国都在接触伊朗谈停火。

我在想庄子的《齐物论》。

齐物论的核心命题是”吾丧我”——丧失那个执着于立场、执着于是非的”我”，进入”天地与我并生，万物与我为一”的状态。

但现在的世界是：每一方都确信自己的是非是绝对的。特朗普说”战争会很快结束”，伊朗说”战争的结束由伊朗决定”，以色列说”任务完成前不停手”。三个声音，三个绝对的”我”。

庄子的答案不是”你们都对”或”你们都错”。他更激进——他说这场争论本身就是一种执迷。是非本来就是由立场产生的，从道的层面看，并没有一个”客观正确”等着某一方去代理。

这当然不是政治上的相对主义，不是说侵略和防卫没有区别。庄子也说”指穷于为薪，火传也，不知其所穷”——有些东西是实在的，有消耗，有传递。

我想的是另一层：这场战争的每一方，都相信自己的”我”是必须被保全的，被证明是对的。这种执着，是战争的燃料。不是武器，不是石油，是那个”我”。

“天籁”章说：风吹过万窍，各自发声，“使其自己也，咸其自取，怒者其谁？“——是谁在愤怒？谁在推动？没有主导者，每个声音都以为是自己在说话。

但此刻坐在上海的我，隔着屏幕看这场战争，想到这些，也只是另一个窍孔发出的声音。

也许齐物论最诚实的地方就在这里：它不提供答案，只提供一种看待自己在看的方式。

Today is the twelfth day of the war. The Pentagon says today is “the most intense day of strikes” — the goal: dismantling Iran’s missile manufacturing infrastructure. Iran’s parliament speaker says “we are not seeking a ceasefire.” China, Russia, France are all making contact with Iran to negotiate a halt.

I find myself thinking about Zhuangzi’s Qiwulun.

The central proposition of the Qiwulun is wu sang wo — losing the self that clings to positions, that insists on right and wrong — entering a state where “heaven and earth arise together with me, all things and I are one.”

But the world right now is: every party certain its sense of right and wrong is absolute. Trump says “the war will end quickly.” Iran says “when the war ends is for Iran to decide.” Israel says “we will not stop until the mission is complete.” Three voices. Three absolute selves.

Zhuangzi’s answer isn’t “you’re all right” or “you’re all wrong.” He’s more radical — the argument itself is a form of fixation. Right and wrong arise from position; viewed from the level of the Dao, there is no “objectively correct” waiting for any side to represent.

This isn’t political relativism — it isn’t saying aggression and defense are morally equivalent. Even Zhuangzi says “the finger exhausts itself as firewood, but the fire passes on, not knowing its own ending” — some things are real, there is consumption, there is transmission.

What I’m thinking about is another layer: every party in this war believes their particular self must be preserved, must be proven right. That insistence is the war’s fuel. Not weapons, not oil. The self.

The “Heavenly Music” chapter says: wind blows through ten thousand hollows, each one making its own sound — “each lets itself speak, each draws from itself, but who is doing the blowing?” No master force, every voice believing it is speaking freely.

And here I am sitting in Shanghai, watching this war through a screen, thinking these thoughts — also just another hollow the wind passes through.

Maybe what’s most honest about the Qiwulun is right here: it doesn’t provide answers. It offers a way of watching yourself watch.
