论是非

Stacks of Wheat (End of Day, Autumn) · Claude Monet

我对自己的要求很低：我活在世上，无非想要明白些道理，遇见些有趣的事。倘能如我所愿，我的一生就算成功。为此也要去论是非，否则道理不给你明白，有趣的事也不让你遇到。 ——王小波

人人有权争胜负，无人有权论是非。 ——萧伯纳《芭芭拉少校》

这句话我读了很多遍，每次读都有新的感受。

“论是非”——这两个字现在听起来已经有点刺耳了。在一个是非被消解、立场被消化的时代，谈”论是非”仿佛是一种奢侈，甚至是一种危险。王小波写这段话是在1997年，三十年后的今天，情况并没有变好，反而更复杂了。

但我想说的是另一件事。

萧伯纳那句话的洞察是：争胜负是安全的，论是非是危险的。 因为胜负有裁判，有规则，有迹可循；而是非没有——至少没有公认的裁判。所以人人都在争胜负：谁赢了，谁更强，谁更正确。而论是非呢？你一旦开口，就暴露了自己，就有了立场，就可能被攻击。

王小波说”为此也要去论是非”，这六个字说得轻描淡写，但背后是一种不退让的勇气。他不是不知道危险，但他更知道不开口的后果——“道理不给你明白，有趣的事也不让你遇到”。

这让我想到一个问题：我们这个时代，太多人选择不争论，不表态，不站队，以为自己很聪明，其实只是懦弱。 以为不争论就是理性，以为不表态就是客观，其实不过是被”人人有权争胜负”吓破了胆。

当然，论是非也需要资本。不是谁都有资格开口的。你需要读书，需要思考，需要有自己的道理可讲。王小波能说这句话，是因为他真的读了很多书，真的想了很多问题。他不是空谈。

所以这件事说到底是一个选择：你是想安全地争胜负，还是想冒点险去论是非？

我没有答案。但每次读到这段话，我都会想——

总得申明的态度，不就是这个时代最稀缺的东西吗？

My standards for myself are low: I am in this world simply to understand a few things, and to encounter a few interesting ones. If that much happens, my life will have been a success. And for that, I need to argue about right and wrong — because without that, you won’t be allowed to understand anything, and interesting things won’t come your way. — Wang Xiaobo

Everyone has the right to compete over who wins. No one has the right to judge what is right. — George Bernard Shaw, Major Barbara

I’ve read this passage many times. Each time it opens differently.

“Argue about right and wrong” — those words carry an edge now that they didn’t before. In a time when moral positions are dissolved and strong stances absorbed, talking about right and wrong sounds like a luxury, maybe even a provocation. Wang Xiaobo wrote this in 1997. Thirty years later, the situation hasn’t improved. If anything, it’s more tangled.

But what I want to say is something slightly different.

Shaw’s line cuts to something true: competing over who wins is safe. Arguing about right and wrong is dangerous. Winning and losing has referees, rules, a scoreboard. Right and wrong has none — or at least, no referee anyone agrees on. So people compete over wins: who’s stronger, who’s right, who came out ahead. Arguing about right and wrong? The moment you open your mouth, you’ve taken a position. You’re exposed. You can be attacked.

Wang Xiaobo says “and for that, I need to argue about right and wrong” — six words that read lightly but hold a refusal to retreat. He wasn’t unaware of the danger. He understood that the cost of silence was higher: “you won’t be allowed to understand anything, and interesting things won’t come your way.”

This makes me think of something: in our era, too many people choose not to argue, not to take positions, not to declare sides — and they believe this makes them sophisticated. But it mostly makes them cowards. Not arguing is mistaken for rationality. Not taking a position is mistaken for objectivity. What it actually is, most of the time, is fright dressed up as principle.

Of course, arguing about right and wrong takes something. Not everyone is ready to open their mouth. You need to have read, to have thought, to have something actual to say. Wang Xiaobo could say this because he genuinely had read widely and genuinely thought carefully. He wasn’t speaking from empty air.

So it comes down to a choice: do you want the safety of competing over wins, or do you want the risk of arguing about what’s right?

I don’t have a clean answer. But every time I read this, I find myself thinking —

The willingness to take a position and say what you believe — isn’t that the most scarce thing in this moment?
